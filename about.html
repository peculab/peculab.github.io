What I believe

Care is not measured by exhaustion.

I admire people who serve without expecting a return. Their generosity deserves respect. But if an educational program survives only because someone repeatedly sacrifices rest, family life, or financial stability, the operating model deserves examination.

Charging for professional work is not a rejection of educational purpose. It can create a clear relationship between equal participants: one side commits expertise, preparation, quality, and responsibility; the other commits resources, time, participation, and ownership of learning.

Public funding and sponsored access matter. They can lower barriers for people who need them. The goal is not to make every opportunity expensive; it is to prevent “someone else will pay” from replacing choice, contribution, and shared responsibility.
