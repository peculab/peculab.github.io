After living in the United States, I began to notice how much less time children in American public elementary schools spend at school compared with children in Taiwan.

Our child arrives at school at 8:30 in the morning. On Monday, Tuesday, Thursday, and Friday, school ends at 3:00 in the afternoon. On Wednesday, dismissal is even earlier, at 1:30.

When a child comes home this early, parents quickly realize that school organizes only one part of the day.

There is still a long stretch of time between dismissal and bedtime.

How that time is used, whether for reading, exercise, music, clubs, exploring interests, completing projects, or simply resting, must largely be decided and arranged by the family.

This has changed the way I understand the common idea that American education is more free.

Freedom does not mean that schools have already prepared a richer life for every child.

Often, it simply means that schools do less and families are expected to carry more.

## Schools Provide the Foundation, and Families Take Over the Rest

One way to describe American public elementary education in terms familiar to people in Taiwan is that schools share responsibility for a portion of foundational academic learning, while much of the remaining learning and daily life returns to the family.

After school, parents are not responsible only for transportation and dinner.

They often also need to ask:

What is my child interested in right now?

What kinds of experiences does my child need?

Which abilities can only be developed outside the classroom?

Which activities genuinely fit my child, rather than merely appearing useful for future admissions?

In this sense, the experience resembles that of some homeschooling families in Taiwan.

In homeschooling communities, parents do not simply hand a child over to a fully managed education system and wait for results. They remain involved by finding resources, arranging activities, reading together, and observing what the child truly needs at that stage.

The difference is that in the United States, this level of family involvement is not always called homeschooling.

It may simply be everyday life.

## The Diversity of American Education Also Depends on Family Resources

American college admissions are often described as more holistic than those in Taiwan.

Students are not evaluated only through examinations. They are also expected to present activities, projects, community participation, leadership experiences, and personal stories.

This system can indeed give children opportunities to demonstrate abilities beyond test scores.

But it also means that families must invest considerable time, information, and resources to help children build those experiences step by step.

When a child wants to begin a project, who helps identify the topic and resources?

When a child wants to join an activity, who provides transportation?

When a child wants to remain committed to an interest over several years, does the family have enough time and financial capacity to support it?

When it is time to apply to college, who helps the student understand those experiences and shape them into a coherent learning story?

Standardized tests may still provide a basic reference point, but what ultimately distinguishes applicants is often the learning history they have accumulated over many years.

This is not as different from Taiwan's application-based admissions system as people sometimes imagine.

The main difference is that the American system places more responsibility for building that learning history on students and their families.

If such a system were placed directly into a Taiwanese social environment that strongly emphasizes procedural consistency and fairness, many people might find it deeply unfair.

Differences in family resources can easily become differences in extracurricular opportunities and application materials.

## Schools in Taiwan Carry Much More Than We Often Recognize

When we live inside one system for a long time, it is easy to see only its problems and overlook how much it does for us.

Public schools in Taiwan are responsible for far more than foundational subjects.

They carry a substantial share of teaching, care, classroom management, homework, assessment, remedial support, admissions information, and parent-teacher communication.

Children spend a long portion of the day at school. Even when parents remain involved, they do not necessarily have to design the child's entire after-school life from the beginning.

From this perspective, public education in Taiwan provides an unusually complete form of public service, almost an all-inclusive one.

Yet the more schools carry, the more expectations and criticism they receive.

People expect schools to address academic performance, character, mental health, daily living skills, special needs, interest development, and fairness in admissions, while also respecting the different values of every family.

We want schools to assume more responsibility, but we also easily criticize them for managing too much, teaching too much, or failing to meet every child's individual needs.

This may be one of the most difficult contradictions the system must bear.

## Anxiety Often Leads Us to Idealize an Unfamiliar Place

I do not believe education in Taiwan is better.

Nor do I believe education in the United States is better.

What interests me more is why we so easily believe that moving into another education system will cause a child's problems to disappear.

When parents feel anxious about academic pressure, testing culture, or the school environment in front of them, it is easy to imagine an unfamiliar system as the answer.

A distant place always appears freer.

We see children completing projects, playing sports, developing interests, and presenting highly individual application stories.

What we may not see is how much time parents spend driving, accompanying, planning, researching resources, and accepting the consequences of every choice.

Freedom is not without cost.

It simply moves responsibilities once carried by the school back into the family.

Likewise, the more structured appearance of education in Taiwan does not mean that it fails to respect children.

It means that the public system carries more shared and foundational responsibilities on behalf of families.

## Wherever We Live, Parents Shape the Meaning of Learning

Education systems certainly influence children.

But whether a child can enjoy learning does not depend only on whether that child lives in Taiwan or the United States.

If parents do not look at knowledge with appreciation, and do not experience joy in understanding something, but continually demand that their children study, it becomes difficult for children to believe that learning itself can be meaningful.

If what parents truly pursue is only elite schools, prestigious careers, income, and socially recognized markers of success, then even within a system that appears more free, children may still be pushed into another form of competition.

In one system, children compare test scores.

In another, they may compare sports, music, leadership, volunteer work, projects, and learning portfolios.

The categories become more diverse, but if the underlying purpose is still to prove that one child is better than another, learning remains painful.

Perhaps the most important task is not to find an education system completely free of pressure.

Such a place may not exist.

What matters is whether parents can demonstrate curiosity about the world in their own lives.

To read, not because there is a test tomorrow.

To learn, not because the experience can be added to a résumé.

To explore a question because understanding the world is itself a source of joy.

## Education Systems Allocate Time, but Families Give Learning Its Meaning

The greatest difference between education in the United States and Taiwan may not be freedom versus restriction, or progress versus backwardness.

It may simply be that responsibility for learning is placed in different locations.

The American system leaves more choices to families and asks them to take on more planning, exploration, and decision-making.

The Taiwanese system places more shared educational responsibility on schools, allowing children from different families to receive relatively complete foundational learning and care.

Both systems have strengths.

Both systems have costs.

Only when we stop trying to rank them can we begin to see what our own families truly need.

Schools can teach knowledge, organize courses, and provide part of the environment in which children grow.

But schools cannot replace parents in helping a child believe that learning is worth loving.

Whether children live in Taiwan or the United States, they ultimately watch how adults live.

They see whether we push ourselves because we are afraid of falling behind, or whether we continue learning because we remain interested in the world.

An education system can allocate the hours in a child's day.

But the meaning of learning still comes from how a family understands knowledge, success, and what it means for a person to grow.
