I have been slowly realizing that understanding a city does not have to begin with its buildings, roads, or commercial districts.

Sometimes, what reveals the character of a city most clearly is how it protects nature and how it allows residents to approach the places that have been preserved.

Beside Lake Washington is a wetland habitat connected to the lake. Homes, roads, and ordinary city life are not far away, yet once we paddled into the waterway, the surroundings quickly became quiet.

The water reflected the sky and the trees. Dense aquatic plants grew along both sides of the channel. Each stroke of the paddle left a line of ripples that gradually disappeared. The outline of the city was still visible in the distance, but directly in front of us was another world, shaped by water, plants, birds, and small animals.

Seeing these two landscapes together changed the way I understood the city.

## A Living Ecosystem Can Remain Beside Everyday Urban Life

This wetland is not located in a remote wilderness far from people.

It exists within reach of the communities around it.

People live, work, and travel nearby, while plants and animals continue to have habitat within the same landscape. Urban development has not completely displaced nature, and nature has not been separated into something that can only be viewed from a distance.

The two exist together.

Roads and buildings may appear on the horizon, while reeds, trees, and birds fill the foreground. Once we entered the channel, the sounds of the city were still present, but they became faint. In their place came the sound of paddles touching water, wind moving through leaves, and occasional bird calls.

I appreciate this kind of urban environment.

Nature is not placed outside daily life. It remains part of the city. People can build their lives here, while plants and animals continue to grow within the same land and water system.

## Once We Enter the Wetland, Observation Begins Naturally

Parts of this habitat do not have conventional walking paths, so the most suitable way to enter is from the water.

Canoe rentals are available nearby, and the city also offers guided programs. The arrangement reminded me of public bicycle rentals beside riverside bike paths in Taiwan: even without owning the equipment, people can use an existing public system to begin exploring.

Paddling slowly through calm water naturally changes the pace of attention.

When movement becomes slower, details that are usually overlooked begin to appear.

We could notice which plants grew closest to the water and which branches extended over the surface. Birds rested in the trees or flew low across the channel. At times, we could see turtles, fish, or small movements within the grasses along the shore.

Children begin to ask questions naturally:

Why does that bird keep returning to the same branch?

Why do turtles rest on fallen logs?

Why do aquatic plants grow more densely in certain places?

Was that ripple caused by the wind, or did a fish swim underneath?

These questions do not require a prepared lesson, and they do not always need immediate answers.

Sometimes learning begins by noticing something and becoming curious about it.

## An Ecosystem Is Not a Scenic Image, but a Network of Relationships

After observing the waterway for a while, children begin to understand that nature is not a static background.

Water movement affects where plants can grow. Plants provide places for insects and birds to rest. Fallen trees may become platforms for turtles. Birds move above the water, fish move below it, and grasses along the shore shelter forms of life that may not be visible at first.

Every living thing occupies a place within the environment.

Even while sitting quietly in a canoe, we can sense that a wetland is not created by a single species. It exists because many forms of life depend on one another.

Children can also begin to understand that protecting a habitat means more than preserving a few trees or individual animals. It means allowing the relationships among water, plants, and wildlife to continue functioning.

This kind of understanding is difficult to gain from a single picture.

When a child sees where a bird chooses to rest, how a turtle uses a fallen log, or how different plants grow according to water depth and shoreline conditions, ecology is no longer only a word in a textbook.

It becomes something happening directly in front of them.

## Public Resources Help Residents Enter Their Own City More Deeply

I also appreciate the way environmental protection and public access are brought together here.

The habitat is preserved, while rentals, safe waterways, and guided programs allow residents to enter, observe, and understand it in an appropriate way.

Nature is not completely closed off, but neither is it left without protection. The design creates a balance between access and stewardship that can be maintained over time.

For families, using these public resources is also a way to understand the city.

Libraries, parks, community centers, recreation programs, and environmental learning spaces each provide different points of entry. One family may begin with reading, another with physical activity, and another with a paddle, a walk, or a guided tour.

The value of public resources is not limited to providing a place or a service.

They also give residents opportunities to build relationships with the environments in which they live.

## Learning Happens Within the Everyday Places a City Chooses to Preserve

The strongest impression from this experience was that urban development and environmental protection do not have to be opposing choices.

A city can continue to function while leaving space for wetlands, waterways, and wildlife. People can enjoy the convenience of urban life while still encountering nature within the rhythm of an ordinary week.

When these places are connected to residents through public resources, children can more easily understand that a city belongs to more than humans alone.

Beyond homes and roads, birds need branches on which to rest, turtles need fallen logs, fish need healthy water, and plants need shorelines where they can grow.

Learning does not take place only in classrooms.

It may happen in a library, a park, or along a quiet waterway.

When a child sits in a canoe and begins to look carefully at a bird, a patch of aquatic plants, or a widening ripple, that child is not only observing nature. The child is also learning how a city can live together with the natural world.

Perhaps one thing families can do is pay closer attention to the public resources already around us, enter these places with our children, and take time to observe, ask questions, and gradually build a relationship with the land and water we call home.
