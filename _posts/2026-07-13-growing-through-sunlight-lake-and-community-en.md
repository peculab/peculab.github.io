Today was the first day of church summer camp.

Our child played soccer and baseball under the sun, swam in the lake, and had a water fight with the other children near the shore. When we picked him up, he excitedly told us what he had learned, what he had eaten for lunch, and what kind of water blaster we should prepare for the next day.

He wanted one that could shoot farther than the ones used by the camp leaders.

All of this may sound very ordinary.

Yet what I saw was more than a child having a happy day. I saw many abilities that are often separated into different school subjects growing together through real life.

## When a Child Truly Wants to Accomplish Something, Learning Happens

To complete a baseball passing drill with his group, he had to notice where his teammates were standing, understand the rules, and communicate clearly.

To control the soccer ball and pass it to a teammate, he needed more than physical coordination. He also had to wait, judge distance, cooperate, and respond to others.

When he wanted to share his work, he was able to approach a camp leader, explain his ideas, and ask for help.

This is what **situated learning** looks like in everyday life.

Language is not first divided into vocabulary lists, sentence patterns, and tests, with the hope that a child may eventually use it. Instead, language becomes meaningful because the child genuinely wants to join a group, complete a task, and make himself understood.

He was not speaking English in order to practice English.

He was speaking because something he cared about could only move forward through communication.

That is precisely why these language experiences are more likely to remain in memory and become abilities he can truly use.

## Experience Is Not Separate from Learning; It Is Learning

Experiential learning reminds us that children do not learn only by understanding an explanation.

They need to act, encounter a problem, observe what happened, adjust, and try again.

If the ball is missed the first time, the child changes where he stands.

If a pass does not reach a teammate, he adjusts the direction or strength.

If changing clothes becomes confusing, he gradually learns a better order for organizing himself.

These experiences may not appear on a worksheet, and they may not earn a certificate. Yet they contain a complete learning cycle: trying, noticing, adjusting, and acting again.

For a child, the body is not merely a container carrying the brain.

Through running, touching, falling, waiting, cooperating, and beginning again, he comes to understand both himself and the world.

## In Community, a Child Learns How to Become Himself

Our child told us that he sometimes prefers to sit quietly by himself during lunch, but he does not reject other children who want to come near him.

I found that deeply meaningful.

Growth does not mean requiring every child to become outgoing. Nor should healthy adjustment mean behaving exactly like everyone else.

True social learning happens when a child gradually understands his own needs while also making room for others.

He can enjoy quiet without closing himself off.

He can appreciate solitude while still learning how to respond when someone approaches.

From a sociocultural perspective, many abilities emerge through interaction with peers and adults. Tasks that a child cannot yet complete independently may gradually become his own through observation, imitation, cooperation, and appropriate support.

Within a group, he is not merely learning to follow rules.

He is learning to express himself, understand others, and find a balance between the two.

## Changing Clothes Independently Is Also an Important Learning Outcome

After swimming, he changed out of his swimsuit, dried himself, put on his clothes, organized his belongings, and waited for us to arrive.

These small acts would probably never appear in a course syllabus, yet they were among the most meaningful signs of growth I noticed that day.

Self-directed learning does not simply mean completing academic materials independently at an early age.

It begins when a child gradually develops the sense:

I can take care of myself.

I know what to do next.

When I encounter a problem, I can try to solve it or ask someone for help.

When children are given some choice, some responsibility, and the experience of successfully completing a task, intrinsic motivation begins to grow.

Even his serious planning about what kind of water blaster to bring the next day involved goals, comparison, strategy, and preparation.

To an adult, it may be only a water fight.

To a child, it is a chance to prepare for something he is genuinely looking forward to.

## In the AI Era, Childhood Experiences Matter Even More

I am not opposed to children learning about AI.

But I am becoming increasingly certain that the most valuable education in the AI era is not teaching children to use more tools at an earlier age. It is giving them enough meaningful life experience to understand what those tools should be used for.

AI can generate language quickly, but it cannot replace the sensation of lake water on a child’s skin.

It can calculate the path of a ball, but it cannot replace the joy of adjusting again and again until a pass finally reaches a teammate.

It can simulate conversation, but it cannot replace the experience of waiting, being misunderstood, trying again, rebuilding connection, and completing something together with another person.

Tools will continue to change.

But bodily awareness, cooperation, resilience, self-care, and curiosity about the world are the foundations that will determine whether a child can use those tools wisely.

## The World Created by God Is Itself a Classroom

The Bible says, “The heavens declare the glory of God; the skies proclaim the work of his hands.”

I believe nature is not merely the background for a camp activity. It is also a learning environment prepared by God.

Sunlight, lake water, grass, wind, friendship, and laughter allow a child not only to know that the world exists, but to experience through his whole body that he is part of creation and is being cared for and shaped within it.

The Bible also tells us that Jesus grew in wisdom and stature, and in favor with God and people.

This reassures me that education has never been only about helping a child know more.

True growth means that wisdom, the body, the inner life, and relationships with God and others are all able to mature together.

When we focus only on how much a child has learned, we may miss the other work God is doing in that child’s life.

Perhaps it is a little more courage.

A little more independence.

One moment of choosing to speak.

One moment of allowing another person to come closer.

These seemingly small changes may be signs that a life is slowly opening and growing.

## For the Rest of the Summer, Let Him Play

He can already read and correctly use the common words expected at the kindergarten level.

For the rest of the summer, I do not want to fill every open space with another class. Nor do I feel the need to prove that he can learn AI earlier than other children.

Let him run, swim, play ball, and have water fights.

Let him use language because he truly needs it, learn cooperation through community, and gradually practice caring for himself through the small tasks of everyday life.

Some knowledge can be learned later.

But a childhood immersed in sunlight, lake water, friendship, and grace only happens once.
